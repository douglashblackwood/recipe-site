The BIR Curry System

A complete BIR method: a pressure-cooked two-component base, one house mix powder, a hard-and-fast fry, and a decoder for turning that single foundation into any curry on the menu.

Based on Al's Kitchen, Misty Ricardo, The Curry Guy (Dan Toombs), Glebe Kitchen & Kris Dhillon
The whole idea in one line Same base + same mix powder → a small, defined set of add-ons per curry. A restaurant plates fifteen curries fast because they are not cooking fifteen recipes, they are applying fifteen small "diffs" to two shared foundations.

1The base — two components, two jobs

The Al's Kitchen approach splits what most recipes lump together. Keep them separate, they do different work.

① The purée — the flavour base

Thick, concentrated blended onion + carrot. This is the bit you fry hard. Because it is a paste, not a watery gravy, it genuinely caramelises (the textbook "keep the base pale" rule is for the dilute one-pot gravy, not this one).

② The onion water — the liquid

The aromatic cooking liquid the onions and carrot pressure-cooked in. It replaces plain water or stock. Added hot, after the purée is fried, then boiled down to the final consistency.

Making the base (batch it — enough for 2 curries)

Into a pressure cooker: 1 kg onions (quartered), 1 medium carrot, 1 small piece cassia/cinnamon, 1 star anise, water just to cover. Pressure cook 12-15 min. Then separate:

Yield check: 1 kg of onions gives roughly 900 g purée (two curries at 450 g each) and a variable amount of onion water. If the onion water runs short of the ~400 ml per curry, top it up with hot water from the kettle, it is aromatic liquid, not a sacred stock.

  • Fish out the whole spices. Blend the solids (onion + carrot) to a thick smooth purée.
  • Keep the cooking liquid as the reserved onion water.
Why these tweaks Carrot = natural sweetness + the orange restaurant colour (so you can cut the added sugar). One medium carrot per kilo of onion, no more or it tastes of carrot. Star anise + onion is a savoury, almost meaty pairing (the pho trick), but it dominates fast, so one only. These live mostly in the base, so every curry inherits a faint warm-spice background, which is pleasant but means the base is "aromatic" rather than perfectly neutral.

The purée colour target

raw / palegoldendeep amber · russet ✓dark · bitter ✗
  • Heat: medium-high, good slug of oil, open pan (no lid, no cartouche, a cartouche steams, the opposite of what you want).
  • Look for: it tightens and darkens to deep amber / russet / light chestnut, and the oil splits out and pools. The oil breaking is the real "done" signal, more reliable than colour alone.
  • Smell: sweet, nutty, caramelised. Sharp or acrid = gone too far.
  • Time: 10-20 min. Eight minutes only softens, the depth comes from pushing past "looks cooked".
  • Rhythm: stir → let it catch → scrape the fond → repeat. Constant stirring prevents browning, walking away burns it.
  • The exception: the mild creamy curries (korma, pasanda) want a pale golden purée, not deep amber. Caramelised base would muddy their colour and fight the cardamom-coconut sweetness. Everything else on the menu gets the full hard fry.
Equipment note Use a pressure cooker / multi-cooker for the softening. But do the hard fry in a wide, heavy pan / karahi on the hottest hob ring. A multi-cooker's sauté is moderate and its deep narrow pot traps moisture, so it steams rather than fries. If staying one-pot: max sear, small batches, patience.

2The mix powder — the house blend

Make one jar, use it for everything. It replaces the per-recipe spice juggling. The building spice (fried early), as distinct from garam masala, the finishing spice (added late, off heat).

3Chicken tikka — the third foundation

The pre-cooked protein pillar. Cooked separately in a wood-fired pizza oven, the closest thing to a tandoor you can have at home, then dropped into any curry off the base. This is the house pre-cooked chicken: any recipe below that says "pre-cooked chicken" means this tikka (the char only adds).

The marinade — the house recipe (1.5 kg diced chicken breast)

  • 1.5 kg diced chicken breast
  • 150 g natural yoghurt (coconut yoghurt for dairy-free)
  • 1 tbsp garlic-ginger paste
  • Juice of ½ lemon
  • 2 tbsp honey
  • A splash of vegetable oil
  • 3½ tbsp tandoori masala powder
  • Orange food colouring (TRS brand)
  • 2 tsp ground coriander · 1 tsp ground cumin
  • 1 tsp turmeric
  • 1 tsp Kashmiri chilli powder · 1 tsp paprika
  • ¼ tsp black pepper · ½ tsp ground cardamom
  • 2 tsp kasuri methi · 1½ tsp dried mint
  • 1½ tsp salt

Method: mix everything, massage into the chicken, cover and refrigerate overnight, ideally 24 hours. Skewer and cook hot and fast (pizza oven technique below).

Source: Al's Kitchen (YouTube), as cooked in this house. Tandoori masala does the heavy lifting; the methi and dried mint are the restaurant tells.

Why the pizza oven wins

Restaurant tikka is defined by the tandoor: 400-500°C, live-fire char, a kiss of woodsmoke. A domestic oven or grill can't touch it. A pizza oven can, so this is more authentic than most home cooks manage.

Technique

  • Raise it off the deck (skewers across a tray / a rack) so heat circulates and chars more than one face. Turn once or twice.
  • Blast and pull. Very hot, short time, char outside, juicy inside. Sitting long to "cook through" dries it out.
  • Keep the char. The blackened edges are the flavour you can't fake in the sauce.
Dairy-free note Al's tikka marinade is yoghurt-based, so as-is it is not dairy-free. For a dairy-free curry (the dairy-free korma or tikka masala), marinate in coconut / dairy-free yoghurt instead, it chars just the same. Otherwise the tikka is a drop-in for tikka masala, korma, makhani, a Saag Balti, or a standalone starter.

4The basic cook — step by step

The "mother" cook (a plain medium chicken curry, serves ~4). Branch it with the decoder in Part 5. Everything slow is front-loaded, so this live cook is a fast, hot 15 minutes.

House standards — read once, applies everywhere
  • Garlic-ginger paste: the Tesco frozen paste, straight from the freezer into the pan (it thaws in seconds at frying heat). "2 tbsp" in every recipe means 2 tbsp once melted, roughly 5-6 of the frozen portions.
  • Chilli powder: the house jar is Kashmiri, which is mild (more colour than fire). All chilli quantities below assume standard hot Indian chilli powder, so with Kashmiri, roughly double the stated amount for the same heat. For the genuinely hot dishes (Madras, Vindaloo, Phal), Kashmiri alone won't get there, top up with fresh green chillies, a hot chilli powder, or Naga pickle. The upside: Kashmiri makes every curry the right colour without food colouring.
  • Salt: 1 tsp per serves-4 curry, in with the onion water at Phase 3 (early salt seasons the sauce as it reduces). Taste at the end and adjust, remembering the sauce concentrates as it boils down.
  1. Phase 0 · ahead Have the mix powder jarred and the base made (purée + onion water separated). Pre-cook the chicken: in this house that means the Part 3 chicken tikka, marinated the day before and blasted in the pizza oven. Set aside. (Making ahead to reheat? Pull it slightly underdone.)
  2. Phase 1 · the Maillard fry Wide pan, hottest ring, 4 tbsp oil. Add ~400-500 g purée (about half the batch). Fry medium-high 10-15 min to deep amber, oil splitting. This is where the flavour is made.
  3. Phase 2 · bloom Splash more oil, fry 2 tbsp garlic-ginger paste 1 min. Stir in 2-3 tbsp mix powder + 1 tbsp tomato purée loosened with a little water. Fry hard 60-90 sec, keep it moving so the powder doesn't catch.
  4. Phase 3 · build Add the per-curry differentiator now (basic version: ½-1 tsp chilli powder). Pour in ~400 ml onion water, hot (cold stalls the fry). Stir smooth. Add the pre-cooked chicken, 1 tsp salt + pinch of sugar.
  5. Phase 4 · reduce & finish Boil hard, lid off, 5-8 min to consistency. Off the heat: 1 tsp garam masala, 1 tsp kasoori methi (crushed in your palms), fresh coriander. Taste, adjust salt / sugar / acid.
  6. Phase 5 · serve or rest Serve now, or cool, rest overnight and reheat gently (low simmer, not another hard fry). Resting usually wins. Never slow-cook the finished curry.

5The decoder — one base, every curry

Add-ons are per single curry (serves ~4), on top of the basic cook. Heat comes from how much chilli powder goes in at Phase 3.

Read this first — the three layers Every curry is built from three layers, not two. (1) Mix powder does ~80% of the spicing in all of them. (2) The finishing trio — garam masala + kasoori methi + fresh coriander — goes into nearly every savoury curry at the end (korma & pasanda being the mild exceptions, which lean on cardamom instead of methi). (3) The extras below are only what is different on top of those two shared layers. So a tikka masala is base + mix powder + the trio + the extras listed, not just almond and sugar.

The dairy-free route to a mild curry The mild curries (korma, pasanda, tikka masala) are exactly the ones blocked by cream, which is why a dairy-free repertoire tends to skew to the hotter non-creamy ones. But in BIR, korma's body comes from coconut + ground almond first, the dairy cream is just a top-up. Swap the cream for coconut cream + ground almond and you get a genuine dairy-free korma that is also mild and child-friendly, off the same base.

6Full recipes — every curry spelled out

Each is complete and standalone, serves ~4, built on the made-ahead base (purée + onion water), mix powder and pre-cooked protein. Bold = what makes this curry distinct, everything else is the shared spine. Method follows the 6-phase basic cook unless the note says otherwise.

Two tiers — why some have a "Dish spices" line and some don't Tier 1 (mix-powder-driven): Madras, Bhuna, Jalfrezi, Pathia, Phal honestly are spiced base + heat + the defining add. The base already carries cumin, coriander, turmeric, paprika, Madras powder & garam masala, so they don't need much more. That's real takeaway practice. Tier 2 (own spice signature): Korma, Dhansak, Saag, Karahi, Ceylon, Rogan Josh and Vindaloo have a genuine dish-specific blend that lifts them above "samey". Those carry a Dish spices line, bloom these with the mix powder at Phase 2 (or temper whole seeds in the oil first where noted).

Mild

Korma Mild serves 4 · dairy-free

  • 4 tbsp oil · 450 g onion-carrot purée · 2 tbsp garlic-ginger paste · 2 tbsp mix powder
  • ½ tsp ground cardamom · ¼ tsp ground cinnamon/cassia
  • no tomato, no chilli · 300 ml onion water (hot)
  • 3-4 tbsp ground almonds · 50 g creamed coconut · 1-2 tsp sugar
  • 550 g pre-cooked chicken · 1 tsp salt
  • Finish: ½ tsp garam masala · fresh coriander (no methi)

Keep it pale, go easy on the purée colour. Stir in almond + coconut + sugar and simmer gently. Sweet and creamy, no heat.

Pasanda Mild serves 4 · dairy-free

  • 4 tbsp oil · 450 g purée · 2 tbsp garlic-ginger paste · 2 tbsp mix powder
  • ¼ tsp ground cardamom
  • no chilli · 300 ml onion water (hot)
  • 3 tbsp ground almonds · 100 ml coconut cream · 1 tsp sugar
  • 550 g pre-cooked chicken (traditionally lamb) · 1 tsp salt
  • Finish: ½ tsp garam masala · coriander (no methi)

As korma but cream-forward and nuttier. Very mild and rich.

Tikka Masala Mild serves 4 · dairy-free

  • 4 tbsp oil · 450 g purée · 2 tbsp garlic-ginger paste · 2 tbsp mix powder
  • 1-2 tsp Kashmiri chilli / paprika
  • 150 g passata + 1 tbsp tomato purée · 350 ml onion water (hot)
  • 100 ml coconut cream · 1 tbsp ground almond · 1-2 tsp sugar
  • 550 g tandoori chicken tikka · 1 tsp salt
  • Finish: 1 tsp garam masala · 1 tsp kasoori methi · coriander

Bloom chilli + mix powder + passata, then add cream + almond + sugar + methi. Drop in the tikka last. Red, creamy, lightly sweet.

Dopiaza Mild serves 4

  • 4 tbsp oil · 450 g purée · 2 tbsp garlic-ginger paste · 2 tbsp mix powder

  • Build exactly as Madras, then go to the limit:

Naga Very hot serves 4 · Al's Kitchen favourite

  • 4 tbsp oil · 450 g purée · 2 tbsp garlic-ginger paste · 2 tbsp mix powder
  • 1-2 tsp chilli powder · 2 tbsp tomato purée + 100 g chopped tomato · 350 ml onion water (hot)
  • 1-2 tbsp Naga pickle (Mr Naga), stirred in at the reduction · pinch sugar to balance
  • 550 g pre-cooked chicken · 1 tsp salt
  • Finish: 1 tsp garam masala · 1 tsp methi · coriander

The pickle is the point, not just the heat. Hot, tangy, garlicky and deep. Build the Naga up gradually to taste.

Naga pickle — the secret weapon (works across the menu) Beyond its own dish, ¼-1 tsp of Naga pickle stirred into a Jalfrezi, Madras, Bhuna or Pathia at the reduction stage adds heat plus tang, garlic and savoury depth that dry chilli powder can't. A flavour move, not just a heat move, this is what Al's Kitchen is doing. Ferociously concentrated, so go small and build, balance with a pinch of sugar. Leave it out of any mild portions. Dairy-free (no dairy issue beyond the heat).

7Technique rules to live by

The two fries are different

Don't conflate them. The purée fries dark (that's the Maillard). The finished sauce is built fast and bright on top. Burnt purée is bitter and unrecoverable, stop when the oil splits.

Hard and fast, not slow

BIR is engineered for a 5-10 min final cook. Slow-cooking the assembled curry dulls the fried-spice hit and dries the chicken. Slow cooking is for traditional from-scratch curries, a different method.

Rest beats instant

Cook hard, then cool, rest overnight, reheat gently. Fat-soluble spices keep infusing and harshness mellows. If reheating, pull the chicken slightly underdone first so it doesn't toughen.

Mix powder vs garam masala

Mix powder goes in early and gets fried (building). Garam masala goes in late, off the heat (finishing). Keeping these two roles separate is the bit most home cooks miss.
